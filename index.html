Scale-Adaptive Photometric Visual Servoing
in 3D Gaussian Splatting

Enlarging the convergence domain by inflating Gaussian scales in 3D

Youssef Alj (UM6P)  ·  Guillaume Caron (MIS, UPJV)
Code Paper (coming soon)

Overview

Key idea: Inflating the scales of 3D Gaussians in a Gaussian Splatting scene model smooths the rendered images, widening the convergence basin of photometric visual servoing (PVS). This enables convergence from initial poses where standard PVS fails, while maintaining sub-centimeter positioning accuracy.

Effect of scale factor α on 3D Gaussian primitives and their splatted rendering. Increasing α inflates the ellipsoids, producing smoother images with wider "power of attraction."

Smoothing Paradigms Comparison

Our approach operates in 3D scene space, unlike prior methods that smooth in 2D image space (PGM-VS) or via optical models (DDVS). This produces depth-dependent, anisotropic, and occlusion-aware smoothing.

Comparison of smoothing approaches. (a) Original PVS. (b) PGM-VS: uniform 2D Gaussian convolution. (c) DDVS: depth-dependent optical defocus. (d) Ours: 3D scale inflation — geometry-aware and anisotropic.

Effect of Scale Factor α

Rendered images for different α values on two scenes: room (indoor) and garden (outdoor). The outdoor scene preserves structure at higher α due to larger depth range.

Cost Function Landscape

Inflating Gaussian scales smooths the photometric cost function, suppressing local minima and creating a convex basin around the desired pose.

2D cost function landscape swept along two translation axes for different α values. The original (α=1.0) has a narrow valley with local minima. At α=2.0, the landscape becomes a smooth convex bowl.

Convergence Experiments

Convergence experiment (pair 14→10, 0.24m, 11.6°). Inflated scales converge ~2× faster than original PVS.

Multi-Pose Convergence Rate

Success rate vs. α for 20 random initial poses. The optimal range is α ∈ [1.8, 2.2], doubling the convergence rate compared to original PVS.
